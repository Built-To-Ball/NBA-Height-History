Built To Ball

A breakdown of the NBA's genetic history

Basketball is a game of endurance, precision, agility, and speed. It takes years of dedication and hard work to become a professional athlete. For basketball however, it can take a little more than just that. Out of the four major professional sports leagues, the NBA is the only league where the players can be spotted at any public venue, simply because of their height. This page is dedicated to exploring the significance of a player's genetic makeup in their ability to make it to the NBA and how it has impacted the game of basketball over the years.

How tall is the average NBA player?

An NBA basketball hoop stands at 120 inches off the floor. The goal of an NBA player is simple, get the ball in the bucket, and prevent the other team from doing the same. This gives a distinct natural advantage to those who are able to reach the hoop better than others. The longer your legs, the higher you can jump, the more ground you can cover. The longer your arms, the higher you can reach and the wider you can touch. It is quite simple to see why people who are taller are more inclined to succeed in the game of basketball. But how big of a distinction is there between the height of an NBA player, and the height of an average man on the streets?

The average American male is 69 inches tall, to put this into perspective, out of the 400+ active NBA players in the league at the beginning of 2018, only one player, Isaiah Thomas, is the same height as that of an average American male. Not a single player is shorter than 69 inches.

Have NBA players always been this tall?

The trend of having tall basketball players dates back to the inaugural season in 1946-1947 when the league was known as the BAA, the Basketball Association of America. Among the 160 players on 11 different teams, only 2 players stood below 69 inches.

The increase in exposure of players around the world, mixed with scientific research into training and optimal nutrition has helped to slowly increase the average height of NBA players from 1947 to the mid 1980's. The average height has stayed relatively stable over the last 20 years. This may be due to several reasons, one of which is certainly the introduction of the three point line in 1980. The three point line has changed the dynamic of basketball and has put more focus on the development of agile and coordinated players rather than those who purely dominate the rim. A shorter guard who is able to consistently hit threes is now worth just as much if not more than a pure center with a much lower three point shooting percentage.

The Big Boys: Introducing Shaquille O'Neal

The NBA may not be getting any taller, but it is sure getting heavier. Shaquille O'Neal is one of the most dominant centers to have played in the NBA. During his rookie year, Shaq was a 7-foot-1 294 lb beast who was almost unstoppable around the rim. Shaq reached his peak during the Los Angeles Lakers three-peat, at which point he was weighing in at over 340 lbs with little to no remorse for anyone who got in his way. The pure dominance of someone as heavy as Shaq, paved the way for heavier players in the NBA. To compensate for the trend of moving towards more agile and cooridnated players, the need for bigger and stronger players who can dominate not just the rim but the entire court increased greatly.

Before Shaq's rookie year, only 2% (59/2692) of all NBA players are listed at over 250lbs. However, in the 25 years since, over 17% (316/1858) percent of all NBA players are listed at over 250lbs.
